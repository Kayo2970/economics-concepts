## What is Economics?

Economics is the study of how individuals, businesses, and governments allocate **scarce resources** to satisfy unlimited wants and needs.

There are two core branches:
- **Microeconomics** — behavior of individuals, households, and firms
- **Macroeconomics** — behavior of the economy as a whole (countries, global systems)

**The Fundamental Problem:** Scarcity. Resources (land, labor, capital, entrepreneurship) are finite. Every choice has an **opportunity cost** — the value of the next best alternative you give up.

> *"Economics is the science of choice under scarcity."*

---

## Microeconomics

Microeconomics studies decision-making at the individual and firm level.

### Core Concepts

| Concept | Definition |
|--------|-----------|
| **Opportunity Cost** | The value of the best alternative forgone when making a decision |
| **Marginal Analysis** | Evaluating the cost/benefit of one additional unit |
| **Incentives** | Factors that motivate behavior (prices, rewards, penalties) |
| **Trade-offs** | Giving up one thing to get another |
| **Rational Choice** | Individuals maximize utility given constraints |

### The Invisible Hand
Adam Smith's idea that individuals pursuing self-interest in free markets unintentionally promote the good of society — prices act as signals that coordinate economic activity without central planning.

## Macroeconomics

Macroeconomics examines the economy at a broad, aggregate level.

### Key Indicators

| Indicator | What It Measures |
|----------|-----------------|
| **GDP** | Total value of goods/services produced in a country |
| **Inflation Rate** | Rate at which the general price level rises |
| **Unemployment Rate** | Percentage of the labor force without jobs |
| **Interest Rates** | Cost of borrowing money (set by central banks) |
| **Trade Balance** | Difference between exports and imports |

## Supply & Demand

The most fundamental model in economics. Prices emerge from the interaction of buyers (demand) and sellers (supply).

### The Law of Demand
> As price increases, quantity demanded decreases (inverse relationship), all else equal.

### The Law of Supply
> As price increases, quantity supplied increases (direct relationship), all else equal.

### Equilibrium
The point where quantity supplied equals quantity demanded — the **market-clearing price**. No shortage, no surplus.

### Shifts in Demand (not just movement along the curve)
- Income changes
- Price of related goods (substitutes/complements)
- Consumer preferences
- Expectations about future prices
- Number of buyers

### Shifts in Supply
- Input costs (wages, raw materials)
- Technology improvements
- Government taxes or subsidies
- Number of sellers
- Natural events

## Market Structures

How competitive a market is affects pricing, output, and efficiency.

| Structure | # of Sellers | Price Control | Examples |
|----------|-------------|---------------|---------|
| **Perfect Competition** | Many | None (price takers) | Agriculture, commodities |
| **Monopolistic Competition** | Many | Some | Restaurants, clothing brands |
| **Oligopoly** | Few | Significant | Airlines, telecom, oil |
| **Monopoly** | One | High | Utilities, patented drugs |

### Monopoly Problems
Monopolists restrict output and raise prices above competitive levels → **deadweight loss** (lost economic efficiency).

## Elasticity

Elasticity measures how sensitive quantity is to a change in price or income.

### Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

```
PED = % Change in Quantity Demanded / % Change in Price
```

| PED Value | Label | Meaning |
|----------|-------|---------|
| > 1 | Elastic | Consumers are sensitive to price changes |
| = 1 | Unit Elastic | Proportional response |
| < 1 | Inelastic | Consumers are insensitive to price changes |

**Inelastic goods:** necessities, addictive products, goods with no substitutes (insulin, gasoline)
**Elastic goods:** luxuries, goods with many substitutes (brand-name cereal)

### Cross-Price Elasticity
- **Positive** → substitutes (Pepsi & Coke)
- **Negative** → complements (cars & gasoline)

### Income Elasticity
- **Positive** → normal goods (demand rises with income)
- **Negative** → inferior goods (demand falls as income rises — e.g., instant noodles)

## Consumer & Producer Theory

### Consumer Surplus
The difference between what a consumer is **willing to pay** and what they **actually pay**.

### Producer Surplus
The difference between what a producer **receives** and the minimum they were **willing to accept**.

### Total Surplus = Consumer Surplus + Producer Surplus
This is maximized under perfect competition — the most efficient outcome.

### Utility
The satisfaction a consumer gets from consuming goods. The **Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility** states that each additional unit consumed gives less additional satisfaction than the one before it.

## Cost & Production

### Key Cost Concepts

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Fixed Cost (FC)** | Costs that don't change with output (rent, machinery) |
| **Variable Cost (VC)** | Costs that change with output (labor, materials) |
| **Total Cost (TC)** | FC + VC |
| **Marginal Cost (MC)** | Cost of producing one more unit |
| **Average Total Cost (ATC)** | TC / Quantity |
| **Sunk Cost** | Past costs that cannot be recovered — irrelevant to future decisions |

### Profit Maximization Rule
Firms maximize profit where **Marginal Revenue (MR) = Marginal Cost (MC)**.

### Economies of Scale
As output increases, average costs decrease. This is why large firms often outcompete small ones.

## Fiscal Policy

Fiscal policy is how **governments** use **spending and taxation** to influence the economy.

### Expansionary Fiscal Policy (Stimulus)
- Increase government spending
- Cut taxes
- Used during recessions to boost demand

### Contractionary Fiscal Policy (Austerity)
- Decrease government spending
- Raise taxes
- Used to cool an overheating economy or reduce debt

### The Multiplier Effect
A $1 increase in government spending can create more than $1 in economic output — because recipients spend it, creating income for others, who spend again.

### Budget Concepts
- **Budget Deficit:** Government spends more than it collects in taxes
- **National Debt:** Accumulated deficits over time
- **Budget Surplus:** Government collects more than it spends

## Monetary Policy

Monetary policy is controlled by a country's **central bank** (e.g., the Federal Reserve in the US, Bank of England in the UK).

### Tools of Monetary Policy

| Tool | How It Works |
|------|-------------|
| **Interest Rates** | Raising rates slows borrowing/spending; lowering rates stimulates it |
| **Open Market Operations** | Buying/selling government bonds to control money supply |
| **Reserve Requirements** | Amount banks must hold vs. lend out |
| **Quantitative Easing (QE)** | Central bank buys assets to inject money into the economy |

### Expansionary vs Contractionary
- **Expansionary:** Lower rates → more borrowing → more spending → growth
- **Contractionary:** Higher rates → less borrowing → less spending → controls inflation

## Inflation

Inflation is the sustained increase in the **general price level** of goods and services over time.

### Types of Inflation

| Type | Cause |
|------|-------|
| **Demand-Pull** | Too much money chasing too few goods ("too much demand") |
| **Cost-Push** | Rising production costs (wages, oil) passed on to consumers |
| **Built-In (Wage-Price Spiral)** | Workers demand higher wages → firms raise prices → repeat |
| **Hyperinflation** | Extreme, rapid inflation (e.g., Zimbabwe 2008, Weimar Germany) |

### Measuring Inflation
- **CPI (Consumer Price Index)** — tracks price changes in a basket of consumer goods
- **PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures)** — preferred by the US Federal Reserve

### The Real vs. Nominal Distinction
- **Nominal** values are measured in current dollars
- **Real** values are adjusted for inflation
- `Real Interest Rate = Nominal Rate − Inflation Rate`

## GDP & Economic Growth

**Gross Domestic Product (GDP)** is the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period.

### The Expenditure Approach
```
GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)
```
- **C** = Consumer spending
- **I** = Business investment
- **G** = Government spending
- **X − M** = Net exports (exports minus imports)

### GDP vs GNP
- **GDP** = production within a country's borders
- **GNP** = production by a country's residents (regardless of location)

### Economic Growth Drivers
- Technological innovation
- Capital accumulation
- Human capital (education, skills)
- Institutional quality (rule of law, property rights)

### The Business Cycle
Economies naturally expand and contract:
`Expansion → Peak → Recession → Trough → Recovery`

A **recession** is technically two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth.

## Unemployment

### Types of Unemployment

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Frictional** | Between jobs, searching for work | Recent graduate job hunting |
| **Structural** | Skills don't match available jobs | Factory worker in an automated industry |
| **Cyclical** | Caused by economic downturns | Layoffs during a recession |
| **Seasonal** | Predictable seasonal patterns | Ski resort workers in summer |

### The Natural Rate of Unemployment
The rate of unemployment when the economy is at full employment — typically 4–5%. Frictional + structural unemployment always exist even in a healthy economy.

### Okun's Law
For every 1% rise in unemployment above the natural rate, GDP falls by approximately 2%.

## International Trade & Globalization

### Comparative Advantage
Countries should specialize in producing goods where they have the **lowest opportunity cost**, then trade. Both countries benefit even if one is absolutely better at producing everything.

### Absolute vs. Comparative Advantage
- **Absolute Advantage** — producing more output with the same resources
- **Comparative Advantage** — producing at a lower opportunity cost

### Trade Barriers

| Barrier | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Tariff** | Tax on imports (raises their price) |
| **Quota** | Limit on the quantity of imports |
| **Subsidy** | Government payment to domestic producers to lower their costs |
| **Embargo** | Complete ban on trade with a country |

### Balance of Payments
- **Current Account** — trade in goods, services, income
- **Capital Account** — investment flows
- **Financial Account** — changes in foreign asset ownership

## Behavioral Economics

Traditional economics assumes people are **rational**. Behavioral economics studies how people actually behave — often irrationally.

### Key Biases & Concepts

| Concept | Definition |
|---------|-----------|
| **Loss Aversion** | People feel losses more strongly than equivalent gains |
| **Anchoring** | Over-relying on the first piece of information seen |
| **Herd Behavior** | Following what others do rather than independent analysis |
| **Sunk Cost Fallacy** | Continuing an action because of past investment, not future value |
| **Nudge Theory** | Subtle changes in choice architecture influence decisions |
| **Hyperbolic Discounting** | Preferring smaller rewards now over larger rewards later |
| **Confirmation Bias** | Seeking information that confirms existing beliefs |

### Key Thinkers
- **Daniel Kahneman** — *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (System 1 vs System 2 thinking)
- **Richard Thaler** — Nudge theory, winner of 2017 Nobel Prize in Economics

## Financial Economics

### Time Value of Money
A dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow because it can be invested and grow.

```
Future Value = Present Value × (1 + r)^n
Present Value = Future Value / (1 + r)^n
```

### Risk & Return
Higher potential returns require accepting higher risk. This is the fundamental trade-off in investing.

### Types of Financial Markets
- **Money Markets** — short-term debt instruments
- **Bond Markets** — long-term debt
- **Equity Markets (Stock Markets)** — ownership shares in companies
- **Derivatives Markets** — contracts derived from underlying assets

### The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)
Asset prices fully reflect all available information. In a fully efficient market, you can't consistently "beat the market."

## Market Failures & Externalities

Markets fail when they don't allocate resources efficiently.

### Types of Market Failure

| Failure | Definition | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| **Externalities** | Costs/benefits imposed on third parties | Pollution (negative), education (positive) |
| **Public Goods** | Non-excludable, non-rival goods markets won't produce | National defense, street lights |
| **Information Asymmetry** | One party has more information than another | Used car sales, insurance |
| **Monopoly Power** | Single seller restricts output, raises prices | Utilities without regulation |

### Correcting Externalities
- **Negative externality:** Tax the activity (Pigouvian tax) — e.g., carbon tax
- **Positive externality:** Subsidize the activity — e.g., education subsidies

### The Coase Theorem
If property rights are well-defined and transaction costs are low, private parties can negotiate to resolve externalities without government intervention.

## Public Goods & Government Role

### Public vs. Private Goods

| | Excludable | Non-Excludable |
|-|-----------|----------------|
| **Rival** | Private goods (food, clothing) | Common resources (fish, clean air) |
| **Non-Rival** | Club goods (Netflix, toll roads) | Public goods (defense, lighthouses) |

### The Free Rider Problem
People benefit from public goods without paying for them → markets underprovide → government must step in.

### Government Functions in the Economy
- Enforce property rights and contracts
- Correct market failures
- Redistribute income (taxes and transfers)
- Provide public goods and merit goods
- Regulate monopolies and anti-competitive behavior
- Stabilize the economy (fiscal and monetary policy)

## Essential Formulas & Rules

```
GDP                  = C + I + G + (X − M)
Real GDP             = Nominal GDP / Price Index × 100
Inflation Rate       = (CPI_new − CPI_old) / CPI_old × 100
Unemployment Rate    = Unemployed / Labor Force × 100
Price Elasticity     = % ΔQd / % ΔP
Total Revenue        = Price × Quantity
Profit               = Total Revenue − Total Cost
Marginal Cost        = ΔTC / ΔQ
Marginal Revenue     = ΔTR / ΔQ
Break-Even Point     = Fixed Costs / (Price − Variable Cost per Unit)
Real Interest Rate   = Nominal Rate − Inflation Rate
Future Value         = PV × (1 + r)^n
Present Value        = FV / (1 + r)^n
Money Multiplier     = 1 / Reserve Ratio
```
